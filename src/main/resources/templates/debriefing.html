Debriefing

We are currently investigating experimental approaches to reduce anxiety. The experimental approaches used in the study are based on models of anxiety, which have shown that anxious people tend to interpret ambiguous situations as threatening. It has been proposed that the tendency to interpret things in a threatening way plays a role in the onset of, and recovery from, anxiety problems. The approaches in this study are designed to either induce healthier interpretations.

Previous laboratory-based studies have shown that reducing the tendency to interpret ambiguous information in a threatening way can reduce anxiety. In the current study, we are evaluating if it possible to administer techniques to change interpretation patterns over the Internet, and whether these programs reduce anxiety. You either participated in a condition that is expected to reduce your tendency to interpret information in a negative way, or you participated in an alternative training condition that may be less effective in changing interpretations.

If you were in a condition designed to change the way you interpret information, you were asked to read and imagine yourself in a series of brief stories. In the main active condition, the last word (or word fragment) gave all brief stories a positive meaning. In one control condition, the last word (or word fragment) gave half of the stories a positive meaning and half of the stories a negative meaning. In the other alternative condition, most of the stories you read were not particularly related to anxiety and were fairly neutral in tone. We expect that the active condition will increase positive interpretations, relative to the other conditions. Additionally, we predict that active training will decrease anxiety symptoms, though this is a new training program, so part of the current study is to determine if this program can work well on the Internet.
